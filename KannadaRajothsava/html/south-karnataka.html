About South Karnataka

South Karnataka's Mysuru region blends royal heritage, Cauvery river landscapes, and living crafts, anchored by icons like Mysore Palace, Brindavan Gardens, the Mysuru Dasara with its elephants, Srirangapatna, Mysore silk, and sandalwood products that define the area's identity.

Visitors are drawn to illuminated palaces, musical fountain shows, temple towns, and a state festival that showcases centuries of Wadiyar‚Äëera tradition and craftsmanship in the city and its surroundings.

Region Snapshot

Centered on Mysuru and the fertile Cauvery basin, the area historically formed the core of the Kingdom of Mysore, with riverine forts and capitals at Srirangapatna shaping the region's political and cultural life. The Wadiyar legacy still frames civic ceremonies and pageantry, culminating each year in the state festival Mysuru Dasara that turns the city into a stage of processions, performances, and palace illuminations.

Brindavan Gardens

Laid out below the Krishnarajasagara (KRS) Dam, Brindavan Gardens is famed for its terraced symmetry, manicured lawns, and evening illumination that make it one of the region's signature attractions. The musical dancing fountain show, synchronizing water, light, and music into a "water ballet," is the star draw for visitors after dusk.

Mysuru Dasara and Elephants

Mysuru Dasara spans nine nights and Vijayadashami day, featuring grand processions with decorated elephants, horses, and cultural troupes that attract lakhs of visitors to the city each year. On Vijayadashami, the traditional Jamboo Savari carries the idol of Goddess Chamundeshwari atop a golden seat on an elephant from the palace to Bannimantap, followed by a torchlight parade that closes the festivities.

Srirangapatna

Srirangapatna, on an island in the Cauvery just north of Mysuru, was a fortified capital where Tipu Sultan fell in 1799 during the Fourth Mysore War, marking a decisive moment in regional history. Silk Weaving

Mysore silk is a celebrated mulberry silk tradition produced by KSIC, with the factory tracing its origins to 1912 under Nalvadi Krishnaraja Wodeyar to serve royal and ceremonial needs before expanding to wider textiles. Karnataka contributes roughly 45% of India's mulberry silk, with Mysuru district a major hub and GI recognition strengthening authenticity and regional identity for Mysore silk.

Sandalwood

Mysuru's sandalwood heritage centers on the prized Santalum album and its Mysore Sandalwood Oil, a protected geographical indication historically regulated by the state due to its economic and cultural importance. The city showcases this legacy through oils, soaps, and carvings and via a dedicated Sandalwood Museum developed by the Forest Department in Mysuru that interprets cultivation and craftsmanship for visitors.
